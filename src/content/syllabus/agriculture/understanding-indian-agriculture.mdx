## Learning Objectives

- Understand the structure of India's agricultural sector, including its contribution to GDP and employment
- Trace the historical evolution of agricultural policy from the planning era through the Green Revolution to contemporary reform efforts
- Identify the key institutional and regulatory constraints that shape agricultural outcomes
- Articulate the classical liberal case for market-oriented agricultural reform

## Overview

Agriculture occupies a paradoxical position in the Indian economy. It employs roughly 42% of the workforce yet contributes only about 18% of GDP — a disparity that reflects persistently low labour productivity and signals the sector's unrealised potential. India possesses some of the world's most fertile land, a diverse agro-climatic profile, and a vast workforce, yet average yields for most major crops remain well below global benchmarks.

The roots of this underperformance lie substantially in policy. From the early decades of independence, Indian agriculture was shaped by a dirigiste framework that included land ceiling legislation, state monopolies over agricultural marketing through APMC Acts, restrictions on inter-state trade and stockholding, and extensive input subsidies. While some of these interventions — most notably the Green Revolution investments in high-yielding varieties and irrigation — produced dramatic short-term gains in food grain output, the broader regulatory architecture has inhibited long-term structural transformation.

Since the 1991 liberalisation, India has dramatically reformed its industrial and services sectors, yet agriculture has remained substantially unreformed. The consequences are visible in stagnant farm incomes, persistent rural distress, and a growing divergence between agricultural and non-agricultural labour productivity. Understanding why agriculture was left behind in the reform process — and what a more liberal policy framework would entail — is essential for any serious engagement with India's development challenges.

## Key Concepts

- **Agricultural dualism**: The coexistence of a large, low-productivity traditional sector alongside pockets of modern, market-oriented farming
- **Input-based vs. outcome-based policy**: The distinction between subsidising inputs (fertiliser, water, credit) and creating institutional conditions for productivity growth
- **APMC regime**: The system of regulated wholesale markets that governs the first sale of most agricultural commodities
- **Land fragmentation**: The progressive subdivision of holdings driven by inheritance patterns, population growth, and land ceiling legislation
