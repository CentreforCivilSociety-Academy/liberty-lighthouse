## Learning Objectives

- Analyse the structure and functioning of India's agricultural marketing system, including the role of APMC mandis and intermediaries
- Evaluate the economic effects of trade restrictions on agricultural commodities, including export bans and import tariffs
- Understand how contract farming, futures markets, and electronic trading platforms can improve price discovery and reduce transaction costs
- Assess the political economy of agricultural market reform and the interests that sustain the status quo

## Overview

The efficiency of agricultural markets determines how much of the consumer's rupee reaches the farmer — and in India, that share has historically been low. Studies across commodity value chains consistently show that farmers receive between 25% and 50% of the final retail price, with the remainder absorbed by intermediaries, transport costs, market fees, and post-harvest losses. This marketing inefficiency is not merely a logistical problem; it is a direct consequence of regulatory design.

The APMC Acts, which govern the first sale of agricultural produce in most states, create a system of geographic monopolies in which a limited number of licensed traders operate within each mandi. The lack of competitive pressure depresses farm-gate prices, while the absence of modern infrastructure — cold chains, grading facilities, electronic weighing — results in post-harvest losses estimated at 10-15% for fruits and vegetables. Reforming this system requires not merely amending individual Acts but rethinking the fundamental relationship between regulation and market access.

Trade policy compounds these domestic distortions. India has frequently imposed ad hoc export bans on commodities such as onions, wheat, and rice, ostensibly to control domestic inflation. While these bans may offer short-term price relief for consumers, they impose severe costs on producers by cutting them off from international markets precisely when global prices are favourable. The unpredictability of trade policy also discourages investment in export-oriented agriculture, preventing India from fully capitalising on its comparative advantages in horticulture, spices, and organic produce.

## Key Concepts

- **Price discovery**: The process by which market prices are determined through the interaction of buyers and sellers — distorted when competition is restricted
- **Value chain integration**: Linking farmers directly to processors, retailers, and exporters to reduce intermediation costs
- **Trade policy volatility**: The use of ad hoc export restrictions and import duty adjustments that create uncertainty for agricultural producers and investors
- **Electronic National Agriculture Market (eNAM)**: A pan-India electronic trading portal that seeks to unify fragmented mandi markets into a single national market
