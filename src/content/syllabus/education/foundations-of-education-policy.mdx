## Learning Objectives

- Understand the constitutional basis for education policy in India, including the 86th Amendment and Article 21A
- Identify the key provisions of the Right to Education Act, 2009 and their intended objectives
- Analyze the distinction between input-based regulation and outcome-based accountability in education
- Evaluate the role of private initiative in expanding access to education across income levels
- Recognize the trade-offs inherent in centralized education mandates

## Overview

India's education policy framework has undergone significant transformation since independence. Education was originally a state subject under the Constitution, but the 42nd Amendment (1976) moved it to the Concurrent List, granting the central government a direct role in policy formulation. The 86th Amendment (2002) inserted Article 21A, making education a fundamental right for children aged 6 to 14, which was operationalized through the Right to Education Act in 2009.

The RTE Act established a comprehensive set of norms governing school infrastructure, teacher qualifications, pupil-teacher ratios, and admissions procedures. While these provisions aimed to ensure a minimum standard of quality, their implementation revealed significant tensions. The Act's input-based approach — requiring specific physical infrastructure and administrative processes — placed a disproportionate burden on small, affordable private schools that had been serving low-income communities effectively despite modest facilities.

This module provides the foundational knowledge needed to critically assess Indian education policy. It encourages participants to look beyond legislative intent and examine actual outcomes — including the unintended consequences of well-meaning regulation on school diversity, parental choice, and learning achievement.

## Key Concepts

- **Article 21A and the 86th Amendment**: The constitutional provision making elementary education a fundamental right
- **Input-based vs. outcome-based regulation**: The distinction between mandating school infrastructure and measuring what students actually learn
- **Budget private schools**: Low-cost, privately operated schools that serve a significant share of India's student population, particularly in urban areas
- **Section 12(1)(c)**: The RTE provision requiring private schools to reserve 25% of entry-level seats for children from disadvantaged backgrounds
