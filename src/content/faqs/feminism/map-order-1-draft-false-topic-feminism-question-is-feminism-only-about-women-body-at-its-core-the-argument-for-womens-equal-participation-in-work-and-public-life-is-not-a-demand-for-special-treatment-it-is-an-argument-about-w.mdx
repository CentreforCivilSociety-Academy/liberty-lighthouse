At its core, the argument for women's equal participation in work and public life is not a demand for special treatment. It is an argument about what a society loses when it excludes half its members from contributing fully. The talents, labour, and ideas of women do not disappear when they are excluded, but they are simply wasted.



*Answering this question honestly requires acknowledging where the perception historically comes from. What is often called first-wave feminism, the movements of the 19th and early 20th century, was built around legal equality: the right to vote, to own property, to work, to enter into contracts. Its logic was that women should be treated as individuals with the same legal standing as men. What is often called third-wave feminism, emerging from the 1990s onwards, took a different turn. It focused less on individual legal rights and more on systemic power, representation, and group identity. Under this framing, feminism became explicitly about women as a collective, defined by shared experiences of exclusion. Some strands of this tradition, particularly radical feminism, go further and argue that women constitute a political class with interests that are distinct from, and sometimes in conflict with, men's.*

*So when people say feminism is only about women, they are often describing a real tendency within certain feminist traditions, accurately. What we argue is simply that this is not the only version of feminism, nor the most coherent one.*

This also matters economically; there could be a [20% potential raise](https://wbl.worldbank.org/en/wbl) in global GDP by closing the gender gap in employment and entrepreneurship. Every household where a woman cannot work, own property, or earn independently is a household with fewer choices and less resilience.

But the argument goes beyond economics. Think about what it would mean if an entire category of people, defined by some characteristic they were born with, were systematically barred from certain jobs, prevented from working at certain hours, or assumed to be incapable of certain tasks. We would recognise that as both unjust and inefficient. The case for women's economic freedom rests on exactly this reasoning. It is beyond the idea of classifying women as a special group, but it is about individuals being treated as individuals.

In India, reformers who fought for women's education and work opportunities often understood this connection clearly. They saw gender exclusion and caste exclusion as expressions of the same problem, i.e. a social order that assigned roles by birth rather than by capacity. Their project was not charity toward women but it was the expansion of freedom itself.

So, in many ways, feminism is not only about women, it is about every individual kept out of an opportunity simply because of the circumstances they were born into.
