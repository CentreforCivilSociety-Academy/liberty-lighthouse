The Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2009 (RTE Act) was enacted with the laudable goal of ensuring universal elementary education. However, a growing body of evidence suggests that several of its provisions have produced outcomes contrary to their stated objectives, disproportionately affecting the very communities the legislation sought to serve.

## Closure of Budget Private Schools

One of the most significant unintended consequences has been the widespread closure of affordable private schools. The RTE Act imposes a set of input-based norms — including minimum playground size, pupil-teacher ratios, and infrastructure requirements — that many low-cost private schools cannot meet. Research by the National Independent Schools Alliance (NISA) estimated that over **400,000 budget private schools** faced the threat of closure or derecognition following the Act's implementation. These schools had been serving millions of children from low-income families who actively chose them over free government alternatives.

## Compliance Burden and Regulatory Overreach

The Act's prescriptive approach to school quality focuses heavily on inputs rather than learning outcomes. Schools must satisfy requirements related to:

- **Infrastructure**: Specific room dimensions, boundary walls, separate toilets, and kitchen facilities
- **Staffing**: Mandated qualifications and ratios, regardless of local context
- **Administrative procedures**: Extensive documentation and reporting obligations

This compliance burden falls hardest on small, community-based schools operating on thin margins, while doing little to address the fundamental challenge of poor learning outcomes in Indian education.

## Reduced Parental Choice

By raising barriers to entry and forcing closures of non-compliant schools, the RTE Act has effectively narrowed the range of schooling options available to low-income families. Parents who had exercised agency by enrolling their children in affordable private schools — often at considerable personal sacrifice — found those schools shuttered by regulation. The result is a system that consolidates children into government schools whose performance, as documented by the *Annual Status of Education Report* (ASER), continues to reflect serious learning deficits.

## The Broader Lesson

The RTE Act illustrates a recurring tension in education policy: well-intentioned regulation can undermine the organic, market-driven solutions that communities develop to meet their own needs. A more effective approach would shift focus from regulating inputs to measuring and incentivizing learning outcomes, while preserving the diverse ecosystem of schools that Indian families depend upon.
