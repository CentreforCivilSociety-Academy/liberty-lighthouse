Education vouchers are publicly funded certificates that parents can use to pay tuition at a school of their choice, including private institutions. Rather than directing funds to schools through government budgets, voucher systems route funding through families, making schools accountable to parents rather than to bureaucracies.

## How Vouchers Work

In a typical voucher system, the government provides eligible families with a voucher worth a specified amount — often equivalent to the per-pupil expenditure in government schools. Parents then "spend" this voucher at any participating school, which redeems it for funding from the state. The essential mechanism is straightforward: **money follows the student**, creating competitive pressure on schools to deliver quality.

## International Evidence

Voucher programs have been implemented in a range of contexts, with instructive results:

- **Chile** introduced a nationwide voucher system in 1981. While the program increased access and school diversity, later research highlighted the importance of complementary measures — such as information transparency and targeted subsidies for disadvantaged students — to prevent socioeconomic sorting.
- **United States**: Programs in Milwaukee, Washington D.C., and Indiana have demonstrated positive effects on graduation rates and parental satisfaction, with particularly strong outcomes for students from low-income households.
- **Sweden** adopted a universal voucher system in 1992, allowing independent schools to receive public funding. Studies documented improvements in average student performance across the system, including in municipal schools facing new competition.

These experiences suggest that vouchers can be effective but must be thoughtfully designed, with attention to information availability, quality assurance, and equity safeguards.

## Application to India

India's existing infrastructure makes it a strong candidate for voucher-based reform. The country already has an extensive network of affordable private schools, and Section 12(1)(c) of the RTE Act — which reserves 25% of seats in private schools for disadvantaged children with government reimbursement — functions as a limited, school-based voucher mechanism.

A broader voucher program could build on this foundation by extending choice to all families, setting reimbursement rates that reflect actual costs, and pairing the program with transparent school performance data. Pilot programs in states such as Rajasthan and Delhi could generate evidence to inform national-scale design, ensuring that Indian families gain genuine access to the educational options they have long demonstrated a preference for.
