School choice refers to the principle that parents — not bureaucracies — should determine which school their child attends. In India, where the quality of government schooling remains a persistent concern, expanding choice is not merely a theoretical preference but a practical necessity for millions of families.

## Parental Autonomy and Agency

The rapid growth of budget private schools across India is itself evidence of parental demand for alternatives. Studies by researchers such as James Tooley have documented that even in some of the poorest communities in Hyderabad, Lagos, and Nairobi, parents chose to pay fees at low-cost private schools rather than send their children to free government schools. This revealed preference reflects a rational assessment: parents value attentive teaching, accountability, and the English-medium instruction that many private schools offer.

Restricting school choice through regulation effectively overrides the informed decisions of families closest to their children's needs.

## Competition and Quality Improvement

When schools must attract and retain students, they face meaningful incentives to improve. A competitive education market encourages:

- **Responsiveness** to parental expectations regarding curriculum, pedagogy, and school culture
- **Innovation** in teaching methods and educational technology
- **Accountability** — schools that consistently underperform lose enrollment and revenue

Evidence from diverse contexts supports this dynamic. In Sweden's reformed system, the introduction of school choice led to measurable improvements in student performance across both public and independent schools. In Milwaukee's voucher program, participating students showed gains in graduation rates and college enrollment.

## The Indian Context

India's education landscape is uniquely positioned to benefit from expanded choice. The country already has a vibrant ecosystem of private schools serving all income levels. Rather than constraining this diversity through input-based regulation, policy reforms could channel public funding directly to families — through vouchers or direct benefit transfers — allowing them to select schools that best serve their children.

Such an approach aligns public investment with parental judgment, creating a system where funding follows the student rather than sustaining institutions regardless of their performance. The evidence from both Indian research and international experience suggests that **empowering families as decision-makers** is among the most effective strategies for improving educational outcomes at scale.
