Contract farming is an arrangement in which a farmer agrees, prior to planting, to produce a specified commodity for a buyer — typically an agribusiness firm, processor, or exporter — at a pre-determined price or pricing formula. The buyer often provides inputs such as seeds, technical guidance, and credit, while the farmer supplies land and labour. The agreement transfers a significant portion of market risk from the farmer to the buyer, providing income predictability that open-market sales cannot guarantee.

## How It Benefits Farmers

Contract farming addresses several of the structural challenges facing Indian agriculture:

- **Price certainty**: Farmers know their selling price before the season begins, insulating them from volatile spot-market fluctuations
- **Access to technology**: Contracting firms typically introduce improved varieties, better agronomic practices, and quality standards that raise productivity
- **Reduced transaction costs**: Direct buyer-farmer linkages eliminate layers of intermediaries, increasing the farmer's share of the final price
- **Credit access**: Input provision by the buyer reduces dependence on informal moneylenders, who charge significantly higher interest rates

## International Precedents

Contract farming is widespread in countries with highly productive agricultural sectors. In Thailand, roughly 30-40% of agricultural output is produced under contract arrangements, particularly in poultry, sugar, and horticulture. In Brazil, contract farming has been instrumental in the expansion of soybean and poultry production. These models demonstrate that when legal frameworks protect both parties, contract farming can accelerate agricultural modernisation without displacing smallholders.

## Regulatory Barriers in India

Despite its potential, contract farming in India has been constrained by regulatory fragmentation. Until 2020, there was no unified national framework — the APMC Acts of various states either prohibited or heavily regulated direct buyer-farmer agreements. The Model Contract Farming Act of 2018, circulated by the central government, was adopted by only a handful of states. The Farmers (Empowerment and Protection) Agreement on Price Assurance and Farm Services Act, 2020 — one of the three farm laws — attempted to establish a national framework but was repealed before meaningful implementation could occur.

The absence of a clear, enforceable legal framework continues to limit the expansion of contract farming, leaving millions of Indian farmers without access to arrangements that could meaningfully improve their livelihoods.
