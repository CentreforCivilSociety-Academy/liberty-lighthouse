Land ceiling legislation, first introduced in the 1960s and 1970s as part of India's broader land reform agenda, imposes statutory limits on the amount of agricultural land an individual or family may own. While these laws were motivated by concerns about equity and the concentration of land ownership, decades of experience reveal that they have contributed significantly to the fragmentation of Indian agriculture and the erosion of farm productivity.

## Fragmentation and Scale

The average landholding in India has declined from 2.28 hectares in 1970-71 to approximately 1.08 hectares by 2015-16, according to the Agriculture Census. While multiple factors drive this trend — including population growth and inheritance patterns — land ceiling laws actively prevent the consolidation of holdings that might reverse it. Over 86% of Indian farmers are now classified as small or marginal, operating holdings below two hectares.

This fragmentation has direct economic consequences:

- **Reduced mechanisation**: Small plots make it uneconomical to deploy modern agricultural machinery, keeping labour productivity low
- **Limited access to credit**: Smaller holdings provide less collateral, restricting farmers' access to institutional finance
- **Inefficient resource use**: Fragmented plots increase input costs per unit of output for irrigation, fertiliser, and pest management
- **Lower investment**: Uncertain property rights and the threat of ceiling enforcement discourage long-term capital investment in land improvement

## The Property Rights Dimension

From a classical liberal perspective, land ceiling laws represent a fundamental restriction on property rights. They prevent voluntary transactions between willing buyers and sellers, distort land markets, and create perverse incentives — including the widespread practice of *benami* (proxy) ownership to circumvent ceiling limits. This undermines the rule of law while failing to achieve the redistributive objectives that motivated the legislation.

## A Path Forward

Reforming land ceiling laws does not require abandoning the interests of small farmers. Rather, it means enabling land markets to function — allowing consolidation through purchase or long-term lease, facilitating farmer producer organisations, and creating institutional mechanisms that let small landholders participate in larger, more efficient farming operations without losing their economic stake.
