India's Agricultural Produce Market Committee (APMC) Acts, enacted by state governments beginning in the 1960s, require farmers to sell designated commodities exclusively through licensed wholesale markets known as *mandis*. While originally intended to protect farmers from exploitative intermediaries, the system has evolved into a set of regulated monopolies that constrain farmer choice and depress farm-gate prices.

## The Problem with Mandis

Under the APMC framework, market committees hold exclusive jurisdiction over the first sale of agricultural produce within their designated areas. This creates several distortions:

- **Limited competition**: Only licensed traders (*arhatiyas*) may purchase within a mandi, reducing competitive pressure on buyer prices
- **High transaction costs**: Commission charges, market fees, and handling costs — collectively known as *mandi tax* — can amount to 8-15% of the sale value in some states
- **Geographic restriction**: Farmers cannot legally sell outside their designated mandi area, preventing them from seeking better prices elsewhere
- **Infrastructural neglect**: Many mandis lack adequate cold storage, grading facilities, and electronic weighing systems, leading to significant post-harvest losses

Studies by the National Council of Applied Economic Research (NCAER) and others have documented that farmers in states with less restrictive APMC regimes — such as Kerala and Bihar, which have amended or repealed their APMC Acts — often receive a higher share of the consumer price.

## The 2020 Farm Laws and Their Repeal

The three farm laws enacted in 2020 sought to create a parallel ecosystem of trade outside APMC mandis, permit contract farming on a national framework, and remove certain commodities from stockholding restrictions. These reforms aligned with longstanding recommendations from the Swaminathan Committee and the National Commission on Farmers. However, sustained political opposition led to their repeal in November 2021, leaving the underlying structural problems unaddressed.

## What Liberalised Markets Would Look Like

A reformed agricultural market would allow farmers to sell to any buyer — processors, retailers, exporters, or other farmers — at any location, at mutually agreed prices. It would encourage private investment in market infrastructure, enable electronic trading platforms, and integrate Indian agriculture more fully into national and global value chains. The objective is not to abolish mandis but to end their monopoly, allowing them to compete on the quality of their services rather than the compulsion of law.
